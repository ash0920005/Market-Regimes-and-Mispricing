## Catching the Breakout

### What's driving the price?
1. Momentum trading relies on the core philosophy that assets exhibiting strong performance in the recent past will continue that trajectory in the short term.
2. We want to programmatically identify **breakouts**: moments when the price aggressively moves outside a defined support or resistance level, typically accompanied by a surge in volume.

### Building the Indicators
We need mathematical ways to quantify this momentum, some popular indicators include Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD), Relative Strength Index (RSI), Rate of Change (ROC), and many others.

For this week, we will focus on implementing MACD and RSI from scratch.


**Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD):** 
A trend-following momentum indicator showing the relationship between two exponential moving averages (EMAs). It is calculated by subtracting the 26-period EMA from the 12-period EMA:

$$MACD = EMA_{12} - EMA_{26}$$

To build the MACD, we first need to understand the **Exponential Moving Average (EMA)**. Unlike a simple moving average (SMA) which gives equal weight to all days, the EMA places a greater weight and significance on the most recent data points, making it more responsive to new information. 

The formula for an EMA at time $t$ is:

$$EMA_t = \left( Price_t \times \frac{2}{N + 1} \right) + \left( EMA_{t-1} \times \left( 1 - \frac{2}{N + 1} \right) \right)$$

Where $N$ is the number of lookback periods.

The MACD line is calculated by subtracting the long-term EMA from the short-term EMA (traditionally 26-period and 12-period):
$$MACD_t = EMA_{12}(t) - EMA_{26}(t)$$

To generate a signal, we calculate a 9-period EMA of the MACD line itself, called the **Signal Line**. 
* **Buy Signal:** When the MACD crosses *above* the Signal Line.
* **Sell Signal:** When the MACD crosses *below* the Signal Line.

**Relative Strength Index (RSI)**
RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the magnitude of recent gains relative to recent losses.

First, calculate the average gain and average loss over a lookback window (traditionally 14 periods):

$$RS = \frac{\text{Average Gain}}{\text{Average Loss}}$$

The RSI is then:

$$RSI = 100 - \frac{100}{1 + RS}$$

The indicator ranges between 0 and 100.

Common interpretations are:
* RSI > 70 → Overbought
* RSI < 30 → Oversold

For our purposes, RSI will act as an additional confirmation signal alongside MACD.

## Filtering the Noise 

Not every signal generated by our indicators is a true breakout. Financial data is notoriously noisy. If we trade on every minor crossover, transaction costs and false starts (fakeouts) will drain the portfolio.

### The Volume Filter

We can write a filter to ensure that any MACD signal is accompanied by a mathematically significant surge in volume. We do this by comparing the current period's volume to a rolling Simple Moving Average (SMA) of the volume over the last $W$ periods. A breakout without a corresponding increase in trading volume is highly suspect. If price is moving aggressively but volume remains low, there may not be enough market participation to sustain the move.

We calculate a rolling Simple Moving Average (SMA) of volume:

$$SMA_t^{Vol} = \frac{1}{W} \sum_{i=0}^{W-1} Volume_{t-i}$$

where $W$ is the lookback window.

We then define a threshold multiplier $c$:

$$Volume_t > c \times SMA_t^{Vol}$$

A signal is only considered valid if the current volume exceeds this threshold.

For example, if a MACD crossover occurs but volume fails this condition, the signal should be ignored.
